# Xperiq Brand Strategy

**Version 1.0 — June 2026**
**Owner: CEO / Brand Steward**

This document covers the strategic layer of the brand: why we exist, what we believe, how we are positioned, and how we communicate that consistently. Every creative, marketing, and sales decision should trace back to something in here.

---

## 1. Brand Purpose

**Why Xperiq exists:**
> Every organisation collects experience data. Almost none of them understand it fast enough to act on it.

The experience management industry was built for large enterprise IT teams with six-figure budgets and six-month implementation timelines. It was never built for the CX Director at a 200-person company, the HR leader running quarterly engagement surveys, or the startup founder trying to understand why customers are churning.

Xperiq exists to close that gap. AI makes it possible for anyone — not just enterprises with data science teams — to have answers from their feedback in seconds rather than weeks.

**The one-sentence purpose:**
> To make world-class experience intelligence available to every team, not just the Fortune 500.

---

## 2. Brand Mission, Vision, and Values

### Mission (what we do every day)
> Turn experience data into clear, actionable intelligence — automatically — for teams of every size.

### Vision (the world we are building toward)
> A world where every organisation understands its customers and employees as well as the best companies in the world do today.

### Brand Values (the 5 principles that guide every decision)

**1. Intelligence over information**
We do not build dashboards that show data. We build systems that explain what data means and what to do about it. Every feature, every Crystal answer, every insight layer should add explanation — not just numbers.

**2. Clarity above all**
Xperiq and Crystal exist to make complex experience data clear. This extends to how we communicate: no jargon, no unnecessary complexity, no ambiguous answers. If Crystal's answer is unclear, that is a product bug. If our marketing copy is unclear, that is a brand bug.

**3. Built for everyone**
We refuse to build for only the enterprise. The Xperiq experience for a 20-person startup should feel as capable and respectful as the experience for a 5,000-person company. "For everyone" is not a marketing claim — it is a product and brand commitment.

**4. Human at every layer**
AI powers everything we build, but Xperiq is not cold, technical, or robotic. Crystal speaks like a knowledgeable colleague, not a report generator. Our marketing feels like it was written by a thoughtful person, not a content factory. People trust what feels human.

**5. Earned confidence**
We are confident in what we have built — but we have earned that confidence by doing the work. We do not oversell. We do not promise what Crystal cannot deliver. When we say "Crystal will answer this," it must be true. Credibility is the brand's most valuable asset.

## 3. Brand Positioning Statement

A formal positioning statement is used internally to make sure every message, campaign, and product decision is consistent. It is not public copy — it is the logic behind the copy.

**Format:** For [target audience], [Xperiq] is the [category] that [unique benefit] because [reason to believe].

**Xperiq positioning statement:**
> For CX, HR, and operations leaders at companies of 50 to 5,000 employees, Xperiq is the AI-powered experience management platform that turns survey and feedback data into clear, layered intelligence automatically — because Crystal, our AI copilot trained on experience science, was designed to answer the questions that matter before you think to ask them.

### Simplified positioning (for everyday use):
> Xperiq is the experience intelligence platform that answers your hardest XM questions automatically — for teams of every size.

## 4. Messaging Hierarchy

When we communicate about Xperiq, we always lead with the most important message and build from there. This hierarchy prevents every team member from saying something different.

### Level 1 — Lead message (say this first, always)
**"Xperiq turns your survey data into answers, automatically."**

This is the simplest, truest thing we can say. Use it in cold emails, pitch openers, social bios, and the first sentence of any press release.

### Level 2 — Proof message (say this second)
**"Crystal AI answers questions like 'Why did NPS drop?' in seconds — with cited themes, confidence scores, and recommended actions."**

This makes Level 1 concrete. It names Crystal, describes a real use case, and differentiates from generic analytics tools.

### Level 3 — Differentiator message (say this third)
**"Unlike Qualtrics or SurveyMonkey, Xperiq was built AI-first — Crystal isn't a reporting bolt-on, it's the product."**

This handles the objection "how is this different from what I have?" Only use this after establishing what Xperiq does — not as the opening.

### Level 4 — For everyone message (say this to close)
**"Enterprise-grade experience intelligence, priced for teams of every size."**

This handles the enterprise-vs-SMB positioning. Lead with this only when the prospect is price-sensitive or has dismissed enterprise XM as "not for us."

### Message hierarchy summary
| Level | Message | When to use |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| 1 | "Turns survey data into answers, automatically" | Always, first |
| 2 | "Crystal answers XM questions in seconds" | Always, second |
| 3 | "Built AI-first, not AI-added" | When competitor comes up |
| 4 | "Enterprise intelligence for every team" | When size/price is a concern |

## 5. Competitive Differentiation

### How to talk about competitors

**The rule:** Never lead with competitor names. Lead with what we do. Only reference competitors when a prospect or journalist brings them up.

**When Qualtrics comes up:**
> "Qualtrics is a great platform for enterprises with dedicated XM teams and IT support. Xperiq is designed for teams who want Crystal-quality intelligence without the implementation complexity or the enterprise price tag."

**When SurveyMonkey comes up:**
> "SurveyMonkey is excellent for collecting data. Xperiq starts where SurveyMonkey ends — the analysis, the intelligence, and the answers."

**When Medallia comes up:**
> "Medallia is built for large enterprise CX operations. We're built for teams who need the same quality of insight but can't wait 6 months to go live or spend $150k per year."

**When someone says "we just use Excel/Sheets":**
> "That's exactly the team Crystal was built for. When Crystal can do in 60 seconds what takes an analyst 3 days, the ROI conversation writes itself."

## 6. Visual Identity Extension — What We Have and What Is Missing

This section fills gaps not covered in `BRAND_GUIDE.md`.

### Photography Style
Every photo used in Xperiq marketing — website, social, slide decks — should follow these principles:

**Subject:** Real people in real work contexts. Not stock-photo-stilted poses. Show people actually looking at data, reacting to insights, having conversations.

**Mood:** Confident and bright. Natural light. Slightly warm tones. Not cold/clinical (this is not a data center company).

**Composition:** Clean backgrounds. The product/screen/data is often a secondary subject — the primary subject is the human reaction to it.

**Treatment:** Minimal post-processing. No heavy filters. No grainy black-and-white (this is a modern tech brand). No generic stock photography of "business handshakes" or "smiling in a meeting room."

**What to avoid:**
- Obvious stock photo aesthetics (perfect lighting, impossible diversity, no one is actually working)
- Robotic or cold imagery that makes Crystal feel scary
- Dark/moody/dramatic photography (we are clear, not mysterious)

**Where to source photography:**
- Take your own (best — most authentic)
- Unsplash (free, high quality, filter for natural/candid)
- Getty Images / Shutterstock (paid, for professional campaigns)
- Hire a photographer for hero images (see `TEAM.md`)

### Illustration Style
If illustrations are used (for explainers, website sections, or social graphics):

**Style:** Geometric, clean, minimal. Consistent with the crystal/facet visual language. Abstract shapes suggesting data, connections, and intelligence — not literal cartoon people.

**Colors:** Use the brand palette only. No additional colors introduced in illustrations.

**Usage:** Illustrations should support text — they do not replace product screenshots or photography. Use sparingly.

### Iconography
- Use Material Symbols Outlined (already in the product) for product UI
- For marketing materials, use a consistent icon set: recommend Phosphor Icons (open source, clean, modern)
- Icons should always be single-color (Xperiq Blue or white)
- Never mix icon sets in one document

### Data Visualization
When showing data charts or graphs in marketing materials:

**Colors:** Use the brand palette for chart colors. Xperiq Blue for primary data series. Teal and Purple for secondary.

**Style:** Clean, minimal axes. No 3D charts. No heavy gridlines. White or very light grey background.

**Typography:** Use Inter for all chart labels and annotations.

## 7. Naming Conventions

### Product feature naming
Features in Xperiq follow a clear pattern: descriptive nouns or verb-noun pairs. No codenames, no internal jargon in public-facing names.

**Good feature names:**
- Crystal (the AI copilot — already established)
- Insight Layers (the four-level analysis system)
- Survey Intelligence (the AI-powered survey analysis view)
- XO-Fusion (the cross-survey analysis feature)
- Workflow Triggers (automation based on survey results)

**Naming rules:**
- Feature names are title case in UI, sentence case in prose
- Never use internal sprint names or ticket numbers as public names
- Crystal AI is always "Crystal" in UI — never "the AI" or "the chatbot"

### Campaign naming
Marketing campaigns use a theme + descriptor format:
- "Crystal Live" (demo campaign)
- "Experience Intelligence Report" (annual research content)
- "For Everyone" (positioning campaign)

### Content series naming
Blog and video series should have consistent, memorable titles:
- "Crystal Answers" (demo video series)
- "Experience Intelligence Weekly" (newsletter)
- "The XM Gap" (thought leadership series)

## 8. Brand Health Metrics

Corporations measure brand health systematically. Xperiq should track these, starting simple and adding depth as the company grows.

### Tier 1 — Track from Day 1 (free tools)

| Metric | Tool | Frequency |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Brand name search volume | Google Search Console | Monthly |
| Direct traffic to website | Google Analytics | Monthly |
| Social follower growth | Native analytics | Weekly |
| Share of voice (mentions vs. competitors) | Google Alerts (free) | Weekly |
| Sentiment of brand mentions | Manual review + Google Alerts | Monthly |
| G2 / Capterra rating | G2.com, Capterra | Monthly |

### Tier 2 — Add at Month 3+

| Metric | Tool | Frequency |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Net Promoter Score (from customers) | Xperiq surveys (use your own product!) | Quarterly |
| "How did you hear about us?" survey | Typeform/Xperiq on sign-up | Ongoing |
| Press coverage count | Manual tracking in spreadsheet | Monthly |
| Backlink growth | Ahrefs or SEMrush | Monthly |

### Tier 3 — Add at Scale (Year 2+)

| Metric | Tool | Frequency |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Aided/unaided brand awareness | Survey panel (Lucid, Pollfish) | Quarterly |
| Brand consideration score | Custom survey | Quarterly |
| Competitor share of voice | Brandwatch ($) | Monthly |

### Monthly brand health scorecard
Create a simple Google Sheet with these columns:
`Month | Website visitors | Social followers (total) | New mentions | G2 rating | "How'd you hear" top source | Brand searches | Notes`

Review this monthly. The trend matters more than any single number.
